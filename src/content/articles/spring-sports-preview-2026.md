Winter isn't done yet — there's a hockey team in Buffalo this weekend that would like a word — but the spring sports calendar waits for no one. Tryouts are wrapping up, rosters are getting posted, and Memorial Field is about to get very busy. Here's where things stand across Garnets spring athletics heading into the 2026 season.

## lacrosse

The headliner. Always is in Rye. The boys' program returns a deep, experienced roster after last year's sectional semifinal run, and the buzz around the team is louder than usual. The midfield group — mostly juniors now — got significant varsity minutes last spring and came back sharper from offseason club ball. The defense brings back three starters who can match up with anyone in Section 1. The goalie competition in practice has been fierce, which tends to make both candidates better. The home opener is March 22 at Memorial Field, with Mamaroneck in week two as an early measuring stick. Circle the Bronxville game in April — that one always means something.

Girls' lacrosse has its own reasons for optimism. The program has been steadily climbing the section standings, and this year's senior class has the kind of stick skills and field IQ that come from growing up in a town where lacrosse culture starts in third grade. If the draw control game is there from day one, this team can compete with anyone on the schedule.

## baseball

The diamond at Rye High gets its share of late-afternoon sun starting this month, and the Garnets baseball program is looking to build on a solid 2025 campaign. Pitching depth is the question every spring — the teams that can throw three or four arms and keep opposing lineups uncomfortable tend to survive the Section 1 grind. Look for the senior class to set the tone early, especially in the first few league games against Eastchester and Harrison. The non-league schedule includes some tests that will show quickly what this group is made of.

## softball

Garnets softball has been quietly competitive in recent seasons, and the program is trending in the right direction. A strong returning core of upperclassmen in the circle and behind the plate gives this team a foundation that a lot of Section 1 programs would envy. The infield should be solid defensively, and if the bats show up the way they did in stretches last year, this could be a team that surprises people who haven't been paying attention. Rye softball deserves more fans at the field — this might be the spring that earns them.

## tennis

Rye tennis, boys' and girls', is one of those programs that consistently performs without generating the headlines that lacrosse and hockey get. The depth chart matters more than the top of the lineup in high school tennis — you win dual matches at third doubles, not first singles — and Rye typically has the kind of roster depth that comes from a town with serious court culture. The Rye Racquet Club pipeline keeps feeding the program, and the coaching staff knows how to build lineups that maximize matchups across the board.

## track & field

Spring track is where you find the athletes who don't fit neatly into a team-sport box — the 400-meter runner who is also the best athlete in the school, the distance crew that trains together through January and February and peaks in May, the throwers and jumpers who are half the football roster moonlighting in spikes. Rye typically fields competitive entries across the sprints and middle distances, and the Section 1 championship meet in late May is always a stage worth watching. Keep an eye on the relay squads — that's where team depth shows up.

## the bigger picture

Spring is when Rye High's athletic culture is on full display. On any given weekday afternoon, there are games and practices running simultaneously across Memorial Field, the tennis courts, the track, and the diamond. It's one of the things that makes this town what it is. The lacrosse opener on March 22 is the unofficial start of the season. Show up early. Bring a chair. It goes fast.
