There's a particular kind of quiet that settles over a storefront when it goes dark. Not the closed-for-the-night quiet, where the chairs are stacked and the lights are off but you know it'll be back in the morning. The other kind. The kind where the sign comes down and the paper goes up in the windows and you realize you forgot to go one last time.

The Snackery Bakeshop at 64 Purchase Street closed in January. Sara Leand, who opened the place in October 2021 and spent four years turning it into one of the most recognizable bakeries in Westchester, announced the shutdown after losing both of her parents. She needed to step away. There was no sale, no transition, no soft goodbye — just a decision that family comes first, which is the kind of thing everyone says they believe until it costs them something real.

And it cost her something real. The Snackery wasn't a side project. Leand built it from scratch — the recipes, the branding, the case full of cookies and pastries that looked almost too good to eat, which never stopped anyone. By January 2025, the Today show had found her, and suddenly a small bakery on Purchase Street was getting national airtime. That's not the kind of thing that happens by accident. That's the result of someone caring about every single detail for years.

What Leand created was the kind of place that became part of the routine without you noticing. You'd stop in after dropping the kids at school. You'd grab a box of cookies before a dinner party because they were better than anything you'd make and everyone knew where they came from. The line on Saturday mornings was its own social event — you'd see the same faces week after week, half of them in athleisure, all of them protective of their spot in the queue.

That's what makes a closing like this different from a restaurant that just didn't work out. The Snackery worked. It more than worked. It became a landmark in four years, which is rare for any business and nearly unheard of for a bakery in a town that already had good options. Leand earned that with consistency and taste, not marketing.

Now 64 Purchase Street sits empty, and everyone has an opinion about what should go there. Another bakery. A wine bar. Something for kids. The speculation started before the paper was fully taped to the glass. That's how it goes on Purchase Street — every vacancy is a referendum on what the town wants to be.

But before the next tenant moves in and the construction permits go up and everyone has a new place to have an opinion about, it's worth sitting with what was there. A woman built something beautiful. People loved it. She walked away because life demanded it. That's not a failure. That's someone with their priorities intact.

If you never made it in, you missed out. If you were a regular, you already know.
