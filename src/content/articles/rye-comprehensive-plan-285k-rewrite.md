Rye's comprehensive plan — the document that governs how every square foot of this city gets used — is 41 years old. The City Council voted on March 4 to spend $285,000 to finally replace it.

That matters more than it sounds.

## what a comprehensive plan actually does

A comprehensive plan is the legal backbone of zoning. It tells the city what can be built where, how tall, how dense, and for what purpose. Every time a developer shows up before the Planning Commission, the comp plan is the document everyone points to. Rye's has been pointing to 1985.

Think about what Rye looked like in 1985. No one was talking about flood mitigation, climate resiliency, or transit-oriented development. The housing market was a different planet. The plan that's supposed to guide this city into the future was written before most current residents moved here.

## who's doing the work

The council hired AKRF, a planning and environmental consulting firm, with Peter Feroe as principal and Corey Block managing the project day-to-day. They're not working alone. OLIN Studio handles parks and open space, Barton & Loguidice covers transportation and infrastructure, and the Pace University Land Use Law Center is running public engagement.

That last one is worth paying attention to. The process includes 14 stakeholder meetings, multiple public workshops, and virtual engagement tools. This isn't a plan that gets written in a conference room and handed down. At least, it's not supposed to be.

## what's on the table

Everything. Land use, housing, flooding, transportation, environmental policy. If you've ever complained about development on Purchase Street, traffic on Midland Ave, or water in your basement after a nor'easter — this is the process where those complaints can actually shape policy.

The project runs roughly 18 months, with a target completion in 2027.

## what to do about it

Show up. The engagement process is designed for resident input and the decisions made in this plan will outlast the next several councils. If you care about what gets built next to your house, what happens to downtown, or how Rye handles the next major storm, this is the meeting to attend — not the one about the variance on your neighbor's deck.

The 1985 plan lasted 41 years. Whatever replaces it will shape Rye for a generation.
