If you felt your coffee mug slide at 10:17 AM on Tuesday, you weren't imagining things. A 2.3-magnitude earthquake hit west of Sleepy Hollow on March 10, and more than 100 people across the Rye area reported feeling the tremor.

No damage. No injuries. The USGS confirmed it, Rye city officials confirmed it, and Westchester County Emergency Services found zero infrastructure issues after surveying the area.

So why does a 2.3 matter?

## the indian point angle

Crews at the decommissioned Indian Point nuclear facility ran precautionary safety surveys after the quake and reported no problems. But County Executive Ken Jenkins used the moment to push back against proposals to reopen the plant, arguing that seismic activity — even minor — underscores why a nuclear facility shouldn't operate in Westchester.

It's a political argument wrapped in a geological event, and it's not going away. Indian Point shut down in 2021, and the debate over its future has been simmering ever since. A 2.3 on the Richter scale won't crack a foundation, but it does hand ammunition to one side of that fight.

## for the record

Earthquakes in Westchester are rare enough to be memorable. This one landed in group chats across Rye within minutes. The epicenter was roughly 20 miles northwest, near Sleepy Hollow, but the shaking carried across the county.

Nothing to lose sleep over. But if your kid asks whether Rye gets earthquakes — now the answer is technically yes.
